## Why a models library

Boardroom conversations about AI and innovation tend to reach for one or two familiar frames — usually a Solow-style productivity story or a Schumpeterian disruption narrative — and stop there. The more useful posture is to know which model is doing the work in any given argument, and to be able to swap frames as the question changes.

This library covers twelve models, grouped into four families: **macro growth foundations**, **innovation-specific mechanics**, the **open-economy frame** that determines why frontier technology lands differently in South Africa than in San Francisco, and the **poverty and inequality lenses** that should be layered on every conclusion above.

The throughline: the same innovation that pushes out the productivity frontier can simultaneously hollow out the labour share and concentrate capital, unless the institutional layer is doing real work.

---

## Family 1 — Macro growth foundations

The stage on which innovation operates. These models shape how boards think about productivity and long-run growth, even when no one names them.

### 1. Solow–Swan growth model

The original neoclassical growth model — output grows from the accumulation of capital and labour, with diminishing returns to each. The persistent puzzle is that observed growth substantially exceeds what capital and labour accumulation can explain. Solow attributed the gap to **technical progress**, which the model treats as exogenous — a "manna from heaven" residual rather than something the economy produces.

**Why it matters for innovation.** Solow established that the *majority* of long-run growth comes from technology, not from saving more or working harder. That single result is the intellectual foundation under every "AI productivity" board paper ever written. Its limitation is also its lesson: by treating innovation as exogenous, Solow tells you it matters but not how to produce it.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Solow is largely silent on distribution. Capital deepening should, in theory, raise wages by raising labour productivity. In practice, whether the productivity gains accrue to workers or to capital owners depends on bargaining power, institutions, and the elasticity of substitution between capital and labour — none of which are in the model. South Africa's post-2000 productivity story shows the limit of Solow on its own: capital deepened, output grew, the labour share fell.

---

### 2. Romer endogenous growth

Paul Romer's central move was to bring innovation *inside* the model. Ideas are non-rival — your use of them doesn't subtract from mine — which means returns to innovation can be increasing, not diminishing. R&D becomes a deliberate, profit-motivated activity, sustained by patents and other mechanisms that let innovators capture some of the value they create.

**Why it matters for innovation.** Romer is the model behind every argument that intellectual property, R&D tax credits, and a thick research ecosystem produce growth. It is also the foundation for thinking about why winner-takes-most dynamics emerge in software and AI — non-rivalry plus network effects plus low marginal cost is a Romerian compounding machine.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** The non-rivalry that drives growth also concentrates returns. The marginal cost of distributing AI inference globally is near zero, but the *fixed* cost of producing frontier models is enormous and falls on a handful of firms. The result is a structural tendency toward rent concentration, with distributional consequences Romer's model itself does not address. Boards designing AI strategies in MEA should read Romer alongside Stiglitz's critique (model 12) — the productivity gains are real, the distributional outcomes are not automatic.

---

### 3. Aghion–Howitt creative destruction

Aghion and Howitt formalised Schumpeter's intuition: growth happens through **quality ladders**, where each innovation makes the previous generation's product obsolete. Incumbents are displaced by entrants. The model predicts that the optimal innovation rate trades off two effects — more innovation today raises growth, but also raises the obsolescence rate that discourages investment.

**Why it matters for innovation.** This is the model behind every "disruption" narrative, but with more rigour than the popular version. Two predictions are operationally useful: competitive pressure raises innovation up to a point (the "inverted-U" relationship), and incumbents systematically under-invest because they cannibalise their own rents. The implication for AI strategy is sharp — the firms most exposed to creative destruction are the ones whose current cash flows depend on the model class about to be disrupted.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Creative destruction is destruction. The *workers* whose firms are displaced rarely follow their jobs into the new sectors. Aghion–Howitt is honest about this — it says nothing about whether the displaced are reabsorbed. The distributional outcome depends entirely on labour-market institutions, retraining capacity, and social insurance systems. In an MEA context, where social insurance is thin, the inequality consequences of disruption are sharper than in a Nordic equivalent.

---

### 4. Acemoglu–Robinson institutional growth

The thesis of *Why Nations Fail* turned into a formal framework: the binding constraint on long-run growth is the quality of **inclusive economic and political institutions** — secure property rights, contestable markets, broad political participation. Extractive institutions produce stagnation regardless of resource endowment or technological access.

**Why it matters for innovation.** This is the model that explains why frontier technology arriving in a country with extractive institutions does not produce frontier outcomes. AI infrastructure, talent, and capital can all be present, and yet innovation diffuses unevenly because the institutional substrate is wrong. For African enterprise leaders, Acemoglu–Robinson is also a warning about *who* sits at the table when AI policy is drafted — extractive policy capture is the failure mode to avoid.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** This is the model's central concern. Inclusive institutions broaden participation in the gains from growth; extractive ones concentrate them. The connection to AI is direct: who owns the data, who owns the models, who sets the rules, and who gets compensated for displacement are all institutional questions, not technological ones. A board that treats AI strategy as purely a technical question is implicitly outsourcing the institutional choices to whoever else is in the room.

## Family 2 — Innovation-specific models

The mechanics of how innovation actually happens — within firms, across networks, and over time.

### 5. Schumpeter Mark I and Mark II

Schumpeter wrote two books with two different theories. The young Schumpeter (Mark I, *The Theory of Economic Development*, 1911) saw innovation as the work of entrepreneurial outsiders disrupting established markets. The older Schumpeter (Mark II, *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy*, 1942) saw innovation as the systematised output of large corporate R&D laboratories. Both are right, in different sectors and different decades.

**Why it matters for innovation.** The Mark I / Mark II distinction explains why innovation policy that works in one industry fails in another. Pharmaceuticals, semiconductors and frontier AI are Mark II — capital-intensive, R&D-systematic, dominated by incumbents. SaaS, fintech and consumer applications are Mark I — entrepreneurial, capital-light, disruptor-led. Mistaking the regime is a category error. South African fintech is mostly Mark I. Frontier-model AI is Mark II globally and, for now, almost entirely outside Africa.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Mark II regimes concentrate gains in capital and high-skill labour, with thin pathways for outsiders to capture rents. Mark I regimes are more permeable but more volatile — the median entrepreneur fails. The inequality consequences of an economy depend on the mix; an economy that is Mark II at the frontier and informal at the base, with thin Mark I middle, looks a lot like South Africa.

---

### 6. National Innovation Systems

Developed by Freeman, Lundvall and Nelson in the 1980s and 1990s, the NIS framework treats innovation as the output of a **networked institutional system**, not a firm-level activity. The relevant unit of analysis is the country (or region): universities, research councils, firms, finance, regulation, intellectual-property regimes, public procurement and labour markets all interact to produce — or fail to produce — innovation.

**Why it matters for innovation.** NIS explains why two countries with similar inputs (GDP per capita, R&D spend, university quality) get very different innovation outputs. The system either composes well or it doesn't. It is the natural framework for asking what an *African* innovation system looks like — what role AfCFTA plays, what role pan-African research networks play, what role diaspora capital plays — none of which fit cleanly in firm-level models.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** NIS is fundamentally about inclusion: who participates in the innovation system, on what terms, with what voice. A system dominated by a handful of large firms produces narrow innovation; one with deep university–industry ties, accessible finance for SMMEs, and broad skills development produces wider distribution of gains. The South African NIS has well-documented gaps — finance for early-stage deep-tech, weak commercialisation pathways out of public research, talent emigration — each of which is a distributional problem dressed as a technical one.

---

### 7. Triple Helix

A specific cut on NIS, developed by Etzkowitz and Leydesdorff: innovation emerges from the **interaction of three institutional spheres** — universities, industry and government — each taking on roles traditionally held by the others. Universities commercialise. Firms do basic research. Governments take equity stakes. The model describes Silicon Valley, Cambridge UK, and Israel's tech ecosystem better than older linear models did.

**Why it matters for innovation.** Triple Helix is operationally useful because it points to specific intervention levers: technology-transfer offices, sovereign innovation funds, university-affiliated incubators, mission-oriented public procurement. It is the model behind the AU's flagship innovation initiatives and behind most national AI strategies, including South Africa's draft policy framing.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Triple Helix can produce concentrated outcomes if the helix is geographically narrow (one university region, one industry cluster, one tier of government). Broadening it — across provinces, across historically disadvantaged universities, across both formal and informal sectors — is itself a distributional choice. Boards engaging with university partnerships should ask which institutions and which geographies are inside the helix and which are outside.

---

### 8. Diffusion of Innovations and the S-curve

Everett Rogers' framework, layered with Bass diffusion modelling, describes how innovations spread through a population over time — from innovators (~2.5%) to early adopters (~13.5%), the early and late majority (~34% each) and laggards (~16%). The cumulative adoption curve is sigmoid: slow start, steep middle, slow finish.

**Why it matters for innovation.** Most boardroom mistakes about AI adoption are timing mistakes. Confusing the early-adopter phase with mass adoption produces premature scaling; confusing the early majority with the laggards produces late entry. The model also underpins forecasting — Bass diffusion gives you a defensible curve to project against, far better than linear extrapolation. For South African enterprises looking at generative AI today, the question is not whether the curve will reach the late majority — it will — but how steep the slope will be and how long the chasm between early adopters and the early majority lasts.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Diffusion is uneven. Innovations reach high-income, high-education, urban populations first and lower-income, lower-education, rural populations last — sometimes by years. The "digital divide" is precisely a diffusion-curve problem, and it compounds: each successive technology widens the gap between those who adopted the previous one and those who didn't. AI is the most consequential of these in a generation. An adoption strategy that treats the curve as inevitable is implicitly accepting the inequality the curve produces.

### 9. Real options theory of R&D

When investment is irreversible and the future is uncertain, the standard NPV calculation systematically under-values projects with optionality. Real-options theory (Dixit, Pindyck, Trigeorgis) treats R&D investments as the purchase of an *option* to invest further, where the option's value rises with the underlying uncertainty. The corollary: under high uncertainty, the right move is often to invest small, learn, and preserve optionality — not to commit fully or to walk away.

**Why it matters for innovation.** This is the right frame for boardroom AI investment, and it is rarely the frame used. The standard discounted-cash-flow analysis penalises AI projects for being uncertain; real-options analysis treats that uncertainty as the *source* of value. Frontier AI investments are option-rich: each pilot expands the surface of what becomes possible. A board that demands DCF certainty before it commits to AI is making a category error — it is asking the wrong tool to answer the right question.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Real-options thinking favours actors who can afford to make many small bets — large firms, well-capitalised startups, governments with patient capital. Capital-constrained actors face a higher cost of optionality and rationally invest less in option-creation, even when the expected value would justify it. This is one of the deeper reasons innovation concentrates: not because incumbents are smarter, but because they can afford more options.

### 10. General Purpose Technology (GPT) framework

Bresnahan and Trajtenberg distinguished a special class of technologies — steam, electricity, the semiconductor, the internet — that **diffuse across the entire economy, generate complementary innovations, and reorganise production**. GPTs are characterised by pervasiveness, improvement over time, and the spawning of innovation in user industries. The productivity gains arrive *late*, after firms reorganise around the technology — Solow's famous "you can see the computer age everywhere except in the productivity statistics."

**Why it matters for innovation.** AI is the GPT framework's current test case. The framework predicts a long J-curve: an initial period of disappointing productivity numbers as firms experiment, followed by a steep ramp as complementary innovations and organisational redesign land. It also predicts that the firms that capture the gains are the ones that reorganise themselves, not the ones that simply buy the technology. For boards, this is the strongest available argument that AI is a strategic and organisational question, not a procurement one.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Historical GPTs have been profoundly unequal in their distributional outcomes — typically benefiting capital and high-skill labour first, with broader gains only arriving after decades of complementary investment, regulation, and institutional adjustment. The electricity GPT took roughly forty years to broadly transform productivity; it took longer for its gains to broadly reach workers. AI will not unfold on the same timescale, but the J-curve and the inequality dynamic likely will. Acting as if the gains will be evenly distributed is acting against the strongest historical pattern in the literature.

## Family 3 — The open-economy frame

These models explain why frontier-tech investment in South Africa is a structurally different problem from the same investment in the United States.

### 11. IS-LM-FX (Mundell–Fleming)

The Mundell–Fleming extension of IS-LM to an open economy with capital mobility produces a sharp result — the **impossible trinity**. A country can have any two of: a fixed exchange rate, free capital mobility, and independent monetary policy. It cannot have all three. South Africa runs a floating rand, has substantial (though imperfect) capital mobility, and uses monetary policy to target inflation. That is a coherent corner of the trilemma — but it has consequences for innovation investment.

**Why it matters for innovation.** Frontier AI infrastructure is priced in dollars. Cloud GPU spend, frontier-model API access, and most enterprise AI tooling are dollar-denominated. A weakening rand raises the local-currency cost of AI investment without any change in its productivity. Mundell–Fleming explains why this is structural, not cyclical: a small open economy with free capital and an independent central bank will see its exchange rate absorb shocks that elsewhere are absorbed by capital flows. Every SA enterprise AI strategy is implicitly a position on the rand.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Exchange-rate volatility hits the poorest hardest, through imported food and fuel inflation. It also widens the productivity gap between firms that can hedge dollar exposure (large, formal, capital-market-connected) and those that cannot (SMMEs, informal sector). The same AI-tooling cost that is a footnote for a JSE-listed corporate is a structural barrier for a township-based fintech. Mundell–Fleming is the macro model behind the inequality of AI access.

### 12. Balassa–Samuelson

The Balassa–Samuelson hypothesis explains why countries with higher productivity in their *tradable* sector also have higher overall price levels — productivity gains in tradables push up wages economy-wide, which raises the price of non-tradables (services, haircuts, housing). Empirically, it explains a substantial chunk of the cross-country variation in price levels and real exchange rates.

**Why it matters for innovation.** AI is a tradables-shifter. As AI raises productivity in tradable services — software, finance, business process outsourcing — Balassa–Samuelson predicts upward pressure on local wages and prices, including in non-tradable sectors that did not see the productivity gain. For South Africa, where the tradables sector is narrow and increasingly tech-led, the implication is that AI-driven productivity gains in fintech and software will gradually pull up the cost structure of the rest of the economy. The mechanism is not bad — it is how rich countries became rich — but it raises distributional questions about who participates in the tradables sector and who pays the higher prices.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Workers in the productivity-gaining tradables sector see real-wage increases. Workers in non-tradables see prices rise without commensurate wage gains. The result is a widening within-country wage distribution, layered on top of any direct AI-related labour displacement. The model is one reason "AI will lift all boats" arguments are weaker than they sound — the tide rises differently in different parts of the harbour.

## Family 4 — Poverty and inequality lenses

These should be layered on top of every model above. They are the diagnostic tools for what the growth and innovation models leave on the floor.

### 13. The Kuznets curve and its critique

Simon Kuznets proposed in 1955 that inequality follows an inverted U as economies develop — rising in the early stages of industrialisation as labour shifts from agriculture to industry, then falling as institutions catch up and the gains broaden. The curve was canonical for forty years. Then the data turned: inequality has risen in the developed world since roughly 1980, contradicting the predicted descending arm. Piketty and others have argued the original curve was an artefact of mid-twentieth-century shocks (wars, depression, postwar institutional rebuilding) rather than a structural law.

**Why it matters for innovation.** The Kuznets curve was the implicit model behind decades of "innovation-led growth will eventually broaden the gains" arguments. Its empirical failure is the reason that argument now needs to be made on its merits, not assumed. There is no automatic descent; if anything, AI-era innovation may accelerate the ascent.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** The lesson of the curve's failure is that institutions, not technology, determine the distributional outcome of innovation. Where institutions adapt — through education, social insurance, progressive taxation, antitrust — the gains can be broadened. Where they do not, inequality compounds. The South African Gini coefficient is among the highest in the world; the institutional layer is doing less work than it would need to.

### 14. Piketty (r > g)

Thomas Piketty's central empirical claim, drawn from two centuries of capital-income data, is that when the rate of return on capital (r) exceeds the rate of growth (g), wealth concentrates. Innovation-driven productivity raises g, which is mitigating; capital concentration in winner-takes-most innovation industries raises r, which is reinforcing.

**Why it matters for innovation.** AI is a near-perfect r > g technology. The fixed costs of frontier-model production are enormous; the marginal cost of inference is near zero; the returns to scale are substantial; and the compounding advantage of data and infrastructure access is large. Piketty's framework predicts wealth concentration as a *direct consequence* of how AI economics work — not as a market failure, but as the equilibrium.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Wealth concentration is the inequality dimension that the growth-rate-focused models systematically miss. Piketty's data show that even in periods of strong productivity growth, capital concentration can rise if r continues to exceed g. The institutional response is well-known — progressive wealth and inheritance taxation, antitrust, broad ownership models — but it requires deliberate choices that most jurisdictions, including South Africa, have not yet made.

---

### 15. Acemoglu–Restrepo task-based model

The standard automation model treats labour as a single factor that can be substituted by capital. Acemoglu and Restrepo broke labour into **tasks** and asked which tasks automation displaces, which it complements, and which entirely new tasks the technology creates. The framework predicts that the labour-share trajectory depends on the balance between displacement (negative) and reinstatement (positive) — the creation of new tasks where labour has comparative advantage.

**Why it matters for innovation.** This is the most operationally useful model in the AI-and-jobs debate. It predicts which roles are most exposed (those defined by tasks AI can do well — routine cognitive, document review, basic coding) and which are least exposed (those defined by tasks involving judgement, embodied skill, social context, or accountability). It also predicts that the *long-run* labour share depends on whether AI mostly displaces existing tasks or mostly creates new ones — and historically, technologies that mostly displace produce falling labour shares.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** The task-based model is the formal underpinning of the "hollowing out" of the middle of the labour market. AI's task profile is unusual — it threatens cognitive, white-collar, mid-skill tasks that previous waves of automation left untouched. The distributional consequences for South Africa, where the formal middle-skill labour market is already small relative to the informal sector, are genuinely unclear and worth research investment, not casual extrapolation from US data.

---

### 16. Sen's capability approach

Amartya Sen reframed poverty and development as a question of **substantive freedoms** — what people are actually able to do and to be — rather than income or utility. The capability approach treats income as instrumental: it matters because it expands capabilities, but other things expand capabilities too (health, education, political voice, security), and a focus on income alone misses them.

**Why it matters for innovation.** The capability frame asks a different question of any AI deployment: what does this expand the capability to do, and for whom? An AI tool that automates a banking process may expand the capability of the bank's customers (faster service, better access for thin-file clients) or contract it (foreclosed services for those the model excludes). The same tool produces different capability outcomes depending on design and deployment. Sen's frame is also the philosophical foundation under most "AI for development" arguments worth taking seriously.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** The capability lens highlights that innovation can reduce income poverty while leaving capability poverty unchanged — or even expanding it, if the technology degrades the capabilities of marginalised groups. Indigenous-language coverage in AI agents is a capability question, not a feature question. Access to AI-assisted healthcare in rural areas is a capability question, not an infrastructure question. For an MEA-headquartered enterprise, Sen's frame is the most useful single lens for distinguishing AI deployments that genuinely serve from those that merely sell.

---

### 17. Stiglitz on inequality and innovation

Joseph Stiglitz's body of work on inequality (most directly *The Price of Inequality*, 2012, and subsequent papers) makes a sharp claim: a substantial portion of modern wealth concentration reflects **rent-seeking** — the capture of value through market power, regulatory advantage, and intellectual-property positioning — rather than productivity-enhancing innovation. The two are difficult to disentangle in aggregate data because both produce outsized returns; the policy response, however, is opposite.

**Why it matters for innovation.** Stiglitz forces a question that growth-focused models avoid: of any given firm's profits, how much reflects genuine productivity contribution and how much reflects rent extraction? In AI specifically, the question is acute. The largest AI firms enjoy enormous returns; the share that comes from frontier-model innovation versus from regulatory positioning, IP enforcement, exclusive cloud arrangements, and data lock-in is genuinely contested. Boards advocating for AI investment should be honest about which of these their strategy depends on.

**Effects on poverty and inequality.** Rent-seeking inequality is bad inequality — it transfers wealth without producing it. Productivity-driven inequality is at least debatable: the gains may eventually broaden, the institutions may adapt. Distinguishing the two is also distinguishing the policy responses: genuine innovation is supported by R&D investment, education, and broad institutional capacity; rent-seeking is curbed by antitrust, IP reform, and regulatory neutrality. Conflating them — which most political debate does — leads to policy that helps neither.

## How to use this library

These twelve models are not a hierarchy, and most board questions touch several of them. A practical heuristic:

1. **Start with a Solow / Romer / Aghion–Howitt frame** to clarify whether the question is about productivity, R&D incentives, or competitive disruption.
2. **Layer in NIS, Triple Helix, or GPT** when the question crosses firm boundaries into ecosystem or policy questions.
3. **Apply Mundell–Fleming and Balassa–Samuelson** when the open-economy implications matter — which, for South Africa, is most of the time.
4. **Always run an Acemoglu–Restrepo and Sen check** on any conclusion before it leaves the boardroom. The labour-market and capability consequences are the questions executives are most often asked to defend.
5. **Use Stiglitz and Piketty as external review** — the questions a sceptical journalist or a hostile regulator would ask.

The goal is not to memorise the models but to know which one is doing the work in any given argument, and to be able to swap frames when the argument shifts.
